Do the "Prosper Risk Assessment Approach" efficient enough?¶

by Abdelkarim Fathi¶

Investigation Overview¶

Through this presentation, I will try to assess Prosper Risk Assessment Approach and develop insights to finally judge this approach's effectiveness. The main features used are Prosper Risk rating, Borrower Rate, Loans' Term, Borrower's Debt to Income Ratio, Borrower's Income Range, and Loans' Amount.

Dataset Overview¶

It's consisting of 113,937 values and 81 features. Some of them are loan features and in the type of categorical data (e.g. LoanStatus, IncomeRange, and Term) and others in form of quantitative data type (e.g. LoanOriginalAmount, and BorrowerRate). It's been passed through cleaning steps to get rid of null rows in some cases and to replace null with more accurate values in others. Also, after considering certain features, it's been reorganized in a more logical shape.

Distribution of different risk classes¶

Almost 50% of cases are in the middle zone, about one-fifth of the listed loan cases labeled as "C" or moderate level of risk following by the "B" and "D" class. While roughly 8% of cases considered as very low of risk "AA" and that is almost equal to the higher level of risk "HR".

Proportion of each loan term¶

More than 75% of loans extended for 36 months while only as few as 14% extend for 12 months.

Different income range slices¶

Middle-income borrowers seized almost 60% of loans then comes the higher income category with about 32%.

Distribution of "Debt To Income Ratio"¶

DebtToIncomeRatio skewed distributionally to the right; 50% of values are between 15% and 31% and mean at 24%.

Average "Borrower Rate" per each loan "Term"¶

On-average the borrower rate for the loan term of 36 months and 60 months are equal that equal almost 19% on average.

"Borrower Rate" distribution per each risk class¶

Lowest risk AA rate values bounded between almost 7% - 9% and around mean of 8% with medium dispersed upward tail. While in the HR cases are totally settled between 30%-32% with long and bold tail downward. They are motivating lower risk borrowers to involve more with a very competitive borrowing rate.

Loan success rate per each risk class¶

The lowest risk class AA has the higher success rate 93%, and the oppostite for the highest risk class HR that has the lowest rate 65%.

Loan loss rate per each risk class¶

Class A has loss rate less than class AA that almost equal to class B, while class HR has the far highest rate 30%.

Loans' Total loss amount per each risk class¶

Interestingly, while class HR has the highest loss rate; almost three times class C, but total amount lost in class C almost three times the amount of class HR, kind of Simpsons-Paradox !!.

Number of succeeded loans for each income range¶

Income range $25,000-49,999 has the highest Completed loans and the second-highest Current loans interchangeably with the income range $50,000-74,999. Both ranges combined seized most of the loans.

Loan success rate per each income range¶

As the income range goes high, the ratio of Completed and Current against each range total loans gets higher as well.

What could be the effect of risk rate and loan term on the borrower rate?¶

Generally Prosper Risk Assessment approach assigned gradually increased Rates for each risk class and the same for each Term within each class.

Regarding class AA through C, loan' rate span increases as the loan Term increases. Moreover, this gap between different loan Term decreased from class to class till it becomes almost equal in class C.

On the opposite, through class D to class HR rates' span for the loans of 36 months time span becomes higher than 60 months long loans. While in class HR all loans are only 36 months time period with the longest rate span between 25% and 35%.

As well, interestingly, rates span for the 36 months' loan are always the longest span within each group. Such as, in class B it's "12.5%-18%", while its "11%-14%" for 12 months' loan and "14%-18.5%" for 60 months loans.

So, Do this risk approach eliminates the total loss amount with considering the income ranges for each class?¶

Generally, income range$25,000-49,999 comes first as the worst performance and commitment regarding the total amount lost while range $50,000-74,999 comes in the second place. Meanwhile, income ranges $1-24,999 and $100,000+ are the best respectively.

Risk class D reached the worst total amount in both mentioned ranges and followed by C and not far class B, It's obvious that there is a money black-hole here that affects investors so hard.

From the total loss amount perspective, class D comes first with losses in just one range of almost $6.5M which is aligned with the before calculated loss rate of 16%. Also, class C comes second again with a high amount in both ranges $25,000-49,999 and $50,000-74,999and related to its high loss rate of 9%.

Conclusions¶

Generally Prosper Risk Assessment approach assigned gradually increased Rates for each risk class and the same for each Term within each class.

In total, income range$25,000-49,999 comes first as the worst performance and commitment regarding the total amount lost while range $50,000-74,999 comes in the second place. Meanwhile, income ranges $1-24,999 and $100,000+ are the best respectively.

As well, I can argue that Prosper Risk Assessment approach needs to manny adjustments to classify borrowers in the most appropriate class of risk to avoid the huge amount of money losses like that being in class D and C and don't count only on borrower rate as appeared here.

Finally, they should consider more the variable of Debt To Income ratio as a main eavaluationg factor in their classification as I noticed that some borrowers of class AA reached a ratio far higher than class HR.
